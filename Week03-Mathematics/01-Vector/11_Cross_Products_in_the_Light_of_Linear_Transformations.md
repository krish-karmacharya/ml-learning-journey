# Cross Products in the Light of Linear Transformations

**Source:** 3Blue1Brown (Essence of Linear Algebra, Chapter 11)

---

## 1. Framing a 3D-to-1D Linear Transformation

To explain why the symbolic cross-product determinant trick works, we must apply the principles of **duality** to 3D space.

Let's define a function $f(\vec{u})$ that takes a variable 3D vector $\vec{u} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}$ as its input, while holding two vectors $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$ constant.

- **The Operation:** The function calculates the volume of the 3D parallelepiped (slanted box) spanned by $\vec{u}$, $\vec{v}$, and $\vec{w}$.
- **The Formulation:** Mathematically, this volume is found by computing a standard 3D determinant:

$$ f\left(\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}\right) = \det\left(\begin{bmatrix} x & v_x & w_x \\ y & v_y & w_y \\ z & v_z & w_z \end{bmatrix}\right) $$

---

## 2. Unmasking the Linearity

This function is a strictly **linear transformation** mapping 3D input vectors down to a 1D number line.

Because it is a linear 3D-to-1D transformation, duality states that there must exist some unique, fixed 3D vector $\vec{p}$ such that applying the function to $\vec{u}$ is identical to taking a standard dot product with $\vec{p}$:

$$ f(\vec{u}) = \vec{p} \cdot \vec{u} $$

$$ \begin{bmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \\ p_z \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = p_x x + p_y y + p_z z $$

---

## 3. Connecting the Algebra to Find $\vec{p}$

If we look at the algebraic expansion of our determinant function along its first column, we get:
$$ x(v_y w_z - v_z w_y) + y(v_z w_x - v_x w_z) + z(v_x w_y - v_y w_x) $$

Lining this up next to our dot product expression ($p_x x + p_y y + p_z z$), we can read off the components of the mystery dual vector $\vec{p}$ directly:
$$ \vec{p} = \begin{bmatrix} v_y w_z - v_z w_y \\ v_z w_x - v_x w_z \\ v_x w_y - v_y w_x \end{bmatrix} $$

> [!important] The Connection
> Notice that these components are exactly the same values generated by the traditional symbolic basis vector determinant trick:
> $$ \det\left(\begin{bmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ v_x & v_y & v_z \\ w_x & w_y & w_z \end{bmatrix}\right) $$

---

## 4. The Geometric Meaning of $\vec{p}$

Now let's ask: What are the geometric properties of this dual vector $\vec{p}$ that satisfy the condition $\vec{p} \cdot \vec{u} = \text{Volume}(\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w})$?

- **Finding the Volume Geometrically:** The volume of a box can be computed by taking the area of its base (spanned by $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$) and multiplying it by its perpendicular height. The height is the projection of $\vec{u}$ onto a line perpendicular to that base.
- **Finding the Volume via Dot Product:** The dot product $\vec{p} \cdot \vec{u}$ projects $\vec{u}$ onto $\vec{p}$ and multiplies the resulting length by the total length of $\vec{p}$.

> [!tip] The Geometric Identity
> For the dot product calculation to match the volume calculation perfectly for every possible input vector $\vec{u}$:
>
> 1.  The vector $\vec{p}$ must be **perpendicular** to the base, ensuring it points in the same direction as the height.
> 2.  The length of $\vec{p}$ must be exactly equal to the **area of the base parallelogram** spanned by $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{w}$.

This vector $\vec{p}$ is the cross product $\vec{v} \times \vec{w}$. The symbolic determinant trick works because it algebraically forces a 3D-to-1D volume calculation to reveal its geometric dual vector.
